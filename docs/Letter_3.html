AMERICAN SONS AND DAUGHTERS OF SWEDEN
John Morton Memorial Building
PHILADELPHIA.


April 4, 1931


Dear Miss von Schulzenheim!

I was ill for nearly two weeks lest month, which has made it very difficult
for me to look after matters here, as they piled up in every direction during
my indisposition.

I made a special trip out to Chicago and the west in reference to the pieces
of sculpture when I found that Mr. Bendix did no take them and I even went as
far as Kewanee to try to persuade Mr. Waller to take one or both of the pieces,
but I had no success. I have now written to M. Bendix, making the situation
clear to him and asking him to reconsider his decision, but I have heard
nothing so far, and I have today written his secretary asking for an early
reply. When I hear something definite I will let you know immediately.

When I first reported the sale of Kings of the Air by Miss Thoresen, she was
apparently pleased and said nothing about not being allowed to sell that
particular copy. I therefore, when I heard from her that she would accept
$ 2000.00, had the piece boxed and sent to Mr. Bendix, and if she has sent him
a second copy that transaction must be between him and her. Such a mixup is,
to say the least, annoying. I have had some trouble with the prices also, as
you know, since you had - in some cases - written one price on the label
placed on the works of art and another price in our catalogue, but I think we
have that straightened that out now.

The stringent financial conditions and the depression here still continue,
and are very much worse than you people in Sweden have any idea of or realize,
and in some instances they are getting worse instead of better. Count Berna-
dotte and Baron Langenskjöld have just completed a trip through American
factories and Baron Langenskjöld told me yesterday that in his opinion times
here would be still worse than they are today. The newspapers are telling a
different story to keep up the courage and morale of the nation. It has
been absolutely impossible to raise sufficient money here to put on a big
celebration in connection with the dedication of the John Morton Building, and
the committee has once again come to the conclusion that that we cannot have the dedi-
cation until perhaps in the fall. I am absolutely frantic about it, but
nothing can be done. Dr. Lewis and I are working on some way of opening
the Exhibit independently of the dedication and making plans for some kind of
publicity campaign, but this is also going to be difficult. The Academy of
Fine Arts has never had such an unsuccessful season in their 128 years of
existence. During their winter exhibition they did not sell a single piece,
which is an unheard of situation in Philadelphia. It is best, therefore, not
to make any definite arrangements for coming here until I can give you the
complete plan.


I have been corresponding with the Brooklyn Museum of Fine arts as well as
other institutions, asking them to take on the exhibit from here. The
Director of the Brooklyn Museum came here personally and inspected the works,
pronouncing them fine indeed. However, he could promise to take only a
portion of the exhibition on account of limited space, and as I could not tell
him whether or not you would agree to this, we have nothing definite with
them.

It has been suggested to me that perhaps a better plan would be to bring the
exhibit to the smaller cities, like Wilmington, Del., Trenton, New Jersey,
and so on, with better success. Mr. Pearson, the manager of the Pearson
Galleries in New York, told me that he had an exhibition recently at which not
a single thing was sold, but that he put on smaller exhibits in other towns
of the state and thus managed to get rid of quite a few sculptures
which he could not sell in New York City.

It is terribly unfortunate that your exhibit should be caught in this gene-
ral world depression, but we are helpless in the matter and I do not know
what to do. I have managed to pay the expenses, the transportation from
New York, custom charges, insurance, etc., which were exceedingly heavy.
We have had a large number of visitors at the Building, who have been much
interested in the exhibit, and I have tried to sell some of the works, but
with little success.

Consul von Dardel is no longer in Chicago, but was removed to Java. It also
appears that Miss Thoresen has written to Mr. Lincoln in Chicago, and I
have also learned that she intends to submit the matter to the Foreign
Department to press it through the Swedish Minister at Washington. This
will do no good whatsoever, and will only serve to antagonize Mr. Bendix
and complicate matters still further.

Regretting that the conditions are so unfortunate, I am

Very sincerely yours,


Amandus Johnson
